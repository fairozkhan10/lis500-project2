Quick definitions (plain language)

Implicit bias is when your brain makes quick associations automatically—sometimes without you realizing it. That doesn’t automatically make someone “bad,” but it can still shape decisions (like who seems “qualified” or “trustworthy”) if nobody stops to question it.

Intersectionality is a way to understand how parts of identity (race, gender, class, disability, etc.) overlap and change someone’s experience. Instead of treating each category separately, it asks how systems affect people who sit at multiple intersections at the same time.

Why this matters in tech

Tech systems often turn human judgments into “rules” (models, policies, ranking systems). If the inputs or assumptions already reflect unequal treatment, the system can scale that inequality fast. That’s why it’s important to slow down, test impact across groups, and create real ways for people to challenge decisions.

Resources (with short annotations)

  • Harvard Project Implicit (IAT tests) — lets you experience how quick associations can show up even when you don’t “mean” them.
  • APA: bias, discrimination, and stress — background context on how bias shows up and affects people over time.
  • NPR Code Switch (race & culture reporting) — examples of how social narratives + institutions shape everyday outcomes.
  • Implicit bias overview (ADA example from health context) — shows how “snap judgments” can affect real decisions in professional settings.

Tip: When you read a resource, ask “who benefits, who is harmed, and what assumptions are treated as normal?”

Connection back to Code & Power

A big theme in this course is that technology is shaped by social power—not separate from it. Implicit bias and intersectionality are useful lenses because they help us see how “neutral” tools can still reproduce unequal outcomes.
